Curriculum Intent

This syllabus is a rigorous documentation of the modern era, organized into 7 fundamental units. Each section contains specific, fact-checked details designed for high-level academic study. Crimson headings denote Main Ideas, and sub-sections follow a numerical hierarchy for maximum organization.

Unit 1: World at War (World War I)

World War I (1914–1918) was a global conflict unprecedented in scale and devastation. This unit examines the causes, experiences, technologies, and lasting consequences of "The Great War."

1.1 Primary Causes of World War I

Historians categorize the causes of WWI using the M.A.I.N. acronym. The immediate spark was the assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand of Austria-Hungary in Sarajevo on June 28, 1914.

M Militarism

  • An arms race, particularly between Britain and Germany regarding naval supremacy, led to inflated military budgets.
  • A culture emerged where war was seen as a valid tool of foreign policy.

A Alliances

  • A web of mutual defense treaties meant a local conflict could quickly escalate into continental war.
  • Triple Entente: Britain, France, and Russia.
  • Triple Alliance: Germany, Austria-Hungary, and Italy.

I Imperialism

  • Competition for colonies and resources in Africa and Asia created friction between European powers.
  • Colonial rivalries heightened tensions and distrust among nations.

N Nationalism

  • Intense devotion to one's nation fueled aggressive foreign policies.
  • The desire for self-determination—particularly among Slavic groups in the Austro-Hungarian Empire—destabilized existing empires.

1.2 Experiences of Troops and Civilians

The experience of the war varied significantly depending on the front and the role of the individual.

Troops on the Front Lines

  • Western Front: Soldiers experienced the grueling reality of trench warfare—defined by mud, disease (like trench foot), and constant artillery threat.
  • Eastern Front: More fluid combat involving vast movements of troops across Russia and Central Europe.

Global Participation

  • Soldiers were drawn from colonies in Africa, India, and Southeast Asia.
  • These troops often faced discrimination despite their vital contributions to Allied and Central efforts.

Civilians & The Home Front

  • For the first time, the "home front" became a target of war.
  • Naval Blockades: Led to starvation, particularly in Germany.
  • Aerial Bombings: Zeppelins brought warfare to civilian populations.
  • Refugees: Millions were displaced across Europe.

1.3 Influence of New Technologies

WWI is often called the first "industrialized war" because of the rapid application of science to killing.

Machine Guns & Heavy Artillery

Forced the war underground into trenches, making traditional cavalry charges obsolete.

Chemical Weapons

Poison gas (chlorine and mustard gas) introduced psychological and physical horror, leading to the development of gas masks.

Tanks & Aviation

Tanks were developed to break trench stalemates; airplanes transitioned from reconnaissance to fighters and bombers.

Submarines (U-boats)

Germany's unrestricted submarine warfare disrupted global trade and was a primary reason for the United States' entry into the war.

1.4 Total War and Its Impact

World War I was a Total War, meaning nations mobilized their entire populations and economies toward the war effort.

State Control

  • Governments took control of industries, set prices, and rationed food and fuel.
  • Centralized planning became essential to sustain prolonged warfare.

Propaganda

  • States used sophisticated media campaigns to maintain public morale.
  • Enemy nations were demonized to sustain support for the war effort.

Societal Shifts

  • With millions of men at the front, women entered the workforce in unprecedented numbers.
  • Women worked in munitions factories and as nurses.
  • This eventually bolstered the movement for women's suffrage.

1.5 The End of the War & Treaty of Versailles

The war ended with an armistice on November 11, 1918, but the subsequent Treaty of Versailles (1919) was deeply divisive.

Group Perception / Impact
Germany Felt humiliated by the "War Guilt Clause" and massive financial reparations ($33 billion).
France / Britain Sought security and revenge; France wanted to ensure Germany could never threaten them again.
United States President Wilson promoted his "Fourteen Points" and the League of Nations, but the U.S. Senate rejected the treaty.
Colonized Peoples Many hoped for "self-determination" as promised by Wilson, but were disappointed when European powers maintained or expanded colonial holdings.

1.6 Primary Causes of the Russian Revolution

The Russian Revolution of 1917 was not a single event but a result of long-standing systemic failures exacerbated by the war.

WWI Failures

  • Russia suffered staggering losses—over 2 million deaths.
  • Severe food shortages destroyed the credibility of Tsar Nicholas II.

Economic Inequality

  • A massive peasantry lived in poverty while a small elite held the wealth.
  • This created fertile ground for Marxist ideologies.

Weak Leadership

  • The Tsar's refusal to grant political reforms alienated the nobility and public.
  • Reliance on controversial figures like Rasputin further eroded trust.

Dual Power & Bolshevik Rise

  • After the February Revolution, a weak Provisional Government competed for power with the Soviets (councils of workers and soldiers).
  • The Soviets eventually supported the Bolsheviks' promise of "Peace, Land, and Bread."

Unit 2: World War II (1939–1945)

World War II was the deadliest conflict in human history. This unit explores the rise of fascist dictators, the innovative and brutal "Blitzkrieg" tactics, and the global struggle for power between 1939 and 1945.

2.1 Rise of Dictators & Pre-war Aggression

Following WWI, weak democratic governments and economic turmoil allowed extremist leaders to seize power in Europe and Asia.

  • Italy: Mussolini's "Black Shirts" established a fascist state by 1922, prioritizing the "Corporate State" over individual rights.
  • Germany: The weak Weimar Republic collapsed under hyperinflation, allowing Hitler to rise to power in 1933 with promises of Lebensraum (living space).
  • Early Aggression: Italy invaded Ethiopia (1935), and Japan overran eastern China, while the League of Nations remained powerless.
  • Spanish Civil War: Served as a "Dress Rehearsal" for WWII; Hitler and Mussolini tested new military technology in the bombing of Guernica (1937).

2.2 The Munich Conference & The Spark of War

The policy of Appeasement reached its peak at the Munich Conference, but it failed to prevent a global conflict.

  • The Munich Pact (1938): Britain and France allowed Germany to annex the Sudetenland in hopes of avoiding war. British PM Neville Chamberlain promised "peace for our time."
  • Invasion of Poland: On September 1, 1939, Germany invaded Poland, marking the official start of WWII in the West.
  • Nazi-Soviet Pact: Hitler and Stalin signed a non-aggression treaty, secretly agreeing to divide Poland between them.

2.3 Blitzkrieg: A New Era of Warfare

Hitler understood that Germany lost WWI because of the slow, methodical nature of trench warfare. He developed Blitzkrieg ("Lightning Warfare") to overwhelm enemies with speed.

01 Softening Up Heavy artillery and airplane strikes to disrupt defenses.
02 Aerial Superiority Airplanes (Luftwaffe) target airfields and communications.
03 The Panzer Spearhead Fast-moving tanks (Panzers) break through the line.
  • The Panzer: These were the fastest and strongest tanks of the time, allowing German forces to invade vast amounts of land quickly.
  • The role of the SS: Following the military advance, SS units would conduct house-to-house sweeps, sending civilians to concentration camps or executing them.

2.4 Soviet Expansion & The Baltic Coast

While Germany struck in the West, Stalin utilized the Nazi-Soviet Pact to expand Russian territory in the East.

  • Annexations: Stalin annexed the Baltic states of Lithuania, Latvia, and Estonia, as well as parts of Romania and the Finnish coast.
  • Occupation Terror: In these newly occupied lands, roughly 10% of the local populations were killed, deported, or placed in camps.
  • The Polish Divide: Germany and Russia divided Poland between them within three weeks of the initial invasion.

2.5 The Fall of Western Europe (1940)

After the "Phony War"—a period where Britain and France waited behind lines like the Maginot Line—Hitler launched a massive spring offensive.

  • Northern Campaign: In April 1940, Denmark and Norway fell in two months, securing Swedish iron-ore and air bases for Germany.
  • The Fall of France: Using fast movements through neutral nations, France fell in just a few weeks (June 1940).
  • Great Britain Stands Alone: With France defeated, Britain became the sole remaining obstacle for Hitler in the West.
  • The US Role: The United States provided weapons to help Britain but remained neutral, wanting to avoid another European "Great War."

We shall fight on the beaches, we shall fight on the landing grounds, we shall fight in the fields and on the streets, we shall fight in the streets, we shall never surrender.

Winston Churchill, June 1940

2.6 The French Resistance & Vichy Government

Following the defeat, France was split into an occupied zone and a collaborating government in the south.

The Vichy Government

A Nazi puppet state in southern France led by Marshal Philippe Pétain. It followed German directives and cooperated with the occupation.

The French Resistance

Led by General Charles de Gaulle from London. Used guerrilla tactics to sabotage German infrastructure and disrupt communication.

2.7 Operation Barbarossa: The Eastern Front

Hitler viewed the Soviets as an "inferior race" due to their historical Mongol ties. On June 22, 1941, he launched Operation Barbarossa, the massive invasion of the USSR.

  • Initial Advance: 3.5 million German soldiers struck quickly, reaching within 18 miles of Moscow.
  • Soviet Response: Though ill-prepared, the Soviets used a Scorched Earth Policy to deny Germans resources as they retreated.
  • Winter Counter-attack: General Georgi Zhukov held off the exhausted Germans as the brutal Russian winter (-18°C) set in.

! Hitler's Strategic Failures

  • Timing: Attacking too late in the spring left the army exposed to the winter freeze.
  • Divided Focus: Hitler ignored generals and split forces between Leningrad, Stalingrad, and Moscow simultanously.
  • Supply Lines: Extended supply lines were constantly sabotaged by partisans.

2.8 Hitler’s "New Order" & The Holocaust

The "Final Solution" was Hitler's systematic, industrial-scale genocide of those he deemed "undesirables," primarily Jewish people.

Legal Persecution

The Nuremberg Laws (1935) stripped Jews of citizenship and banned marriage between Jews and non-Jews.

Escalation (Kristallnacht)

1938's "Night of Broken Glass" saw the destruction of Jewish homes, businesses, and synagogues across Germany.

Ghettos & Concentration

Jews were forced into cramped, segregated neighborhoods before being "liquidated" into forced labor or death camps.

The Death Camps

Infamous sites like Auschwitz utilized gas chambers and crematoriums, killing over 6,000 people per day.

In addition to the 6 million Jews murdered, the Nazis targeted the physically disabled, gay men, and political dissidents. Notorious physicians like Josef Mengele conducted inhumane medical experiments at Auschwitz to support Nazi racial theories.

2.9 Jewish Resistance & The Warsaw Ghetto

Despite extreme danger, many resisted the Nazi regime through armed revolt and hidden acts of courage.

  • Warsaw Ghetto Uprising (1943): Jews took over the ghetto and fought for a month against Nazi forces before being crushed.
  • The Partisans: Over 25,000 Jews joined resistance units in forests, sabotaging German efforts.
  • Individual Acts: Anne Frank's "silent resistance" and the efforts of nations like Denmark, which saved 7,000 Jews by ferrying them to Sweden.

2.10 Total War & The Global Home Front

Allied nations committed to Total War, dedicating every economic and societal resource to the conflict.

Economic Control

Governments rationed vital goods (rubber, fuel, food) and ordered car factories to produce tanks and planes.

Women in Service

Women replaced men in munitions factories and served in auxiliary roles like decoding and ambulance driving.

Restriction of Rights

Under pressure, the US forced Japanese Americans into internment camps; Britain confined German and Italian residents.

War Bonds

Citizens funded the massive military budgets through government-sponsored war bond drives.

2.11 Turning the Tide (1942–1943)

Key victories across three major fronts—The Pacific, North Africa, and the Soviet Union—shifted the momentum toward the Allies.

Theater Key Battle / Turning Point Impact
The Pacific Battle of the Coral Sea First major Japanese setback; battle fought entirely by aircraft carriers and planes.
North Africa El Alamein / Operation Torch Montgomery and Eisenhower defeated Rommel, allowing Allies to cross into Italy.
Soviet Union Battle of Stalingrad Hitler's greatest defeat; the 6th Army was destroyed, and Germans were driven back toward Berlin.

2.12 The Final Stages: D-Day and Yalta

The final push to liberate Europe began with the largest amphibious invasion in history and diplomatic planning for the post-war world.

JUNE 6 D-Day Invasion 156,000 Allied troops storm the beaches of Normandy, France.
AUG 25 Liberation of Paris Allied forces and Resistance fighters free the French capital from Nazi rule.
FEB 45 Yalta Conference Roosevelt, Churchill, and Stalin meet to discuss the final stages of the war.
  • Air Supremacy: Constant Allied bombing missions targeted German factories and defenses ahead of the invasion.
  • The Race to Berlin: Soviet forces reached Berlin from the East before the Western Allies arrived, securing the city.
  • Aftermath: Survivors live to warn the world of the tragedy, as similar genocides have occurred in Rwanda, Cambodia, and the Balkans.

Unit 3: The Cold War (1945–1991)

The Cold War was a 45-year ideological struggle between the democratic United States and the communist Soviet Union. It brought the world to the brink of nuclear destruction and transformed global politics through proxy wars and reform movements.

3.1 Superpower Rivalry & The Iron Curtain

Berlin became the central flashpoint of Cold War tensions, physically dividing Europe into democratic West and communist East.

  • The Berlin Wall: A massive concrete barrier created to prevent low-paid East Germans from fleeing to the democratic West.
  • Hungarian Uprising (1956): Imre Nagy Gain power, ended one-party rule, and threw out Soviet troops before being crushed by the USSR.
  • Polish Unrest: Economic woes in Poland led to repeated riots and strikes against Soviet-backed control.

3.2 The Nuclear Arms Race & Detente

Mutually Assured Destruction (MAD) kept the superpowers from direct conflict, leading to an era of high-stakes diplomacy.

Deadly Escalation

Both sides developed hydrogen bombs and vast stockpiles of nuclear weapons, enough to destroy civilization several times over.

Arms Reduction

To reduce the threat, the US and USSR negotiated SALT (Strategic Arms Limitation Talks) and START (Reduction) treaties.

Era of Detente

A period of "relaxation of tensions" in the 1970s where leaders promoted cooperation and trade.

End of Detente

The 1979 Soviet invasion of Afghanistan abruptly ended Detente, as the US funneled weapons to Afghan insurgents.

3.3 Cold War in Latin America: Cuba

The United States viewed reform movements in Latin America as communist threats, with Cuba becoming the center of attention.

  • The Cuban Revolution: Fidel Castro organized a guerrilla army to overthrow the corrupt dictator and imposed harsh authoritarian rule.
  • The Missile Crisis (1962): The threat of Soviet nuclear bases 90 miles from Florida led to a naval blockade by President Kennedy.
  • The Compromise: Soviets agreed to remove missiles on the condition that the US would not invade Cuba.

3.4 Proxy Wars in Asia: The Korean War (1950–1953)

The Korean War was the first Hot War of the Cold War era, changing Western perceptions of communist expansion.

INVASION June 1950 North Korea (Kim Il Sung) invades the South (Syngman Rhee).
COUNTER Inchon Landing MacArthur's amphibious attack pushes North Koreans back.
STALEMATE 1953 Armistice War ends with 4 million dead; the DMZ remains strictly guarded.

President Truman refused to use atomic weapons, fearing WWIII, and famously fired General MacArthur for public disagreement over war strategy.

3.5 The Vietnam Conflict (1946–1975)

Vietnam was a 30-year conflict fought in two phases: first against French colonialism, then as a Cold War proxy war involving the US.

Event Details Guerrilla Warfare Rebels in the countryside utilized the Ho Chi Minh Trail for supplies. Gulf of Tonkin (1964) Gave the US President broad war powers; led to 550,000 troops by 1968. Tet Offensive (1968) A massive communist attack that damaged US credibility and public support. Fall of Saigon (1975) Saigon falls to the North; Vietnam is unified under communism.

3.6 Soviet Command Economy & Internal Life

The Soviet Union controlled most aspects of public life, valuing obedience and economic security over individual freedom.

  • Inefficiency: A huge bureaucracy decided production rather than supply and demand, leading to waste and consumer shortages.
  • Agricultural Failure: Collectivized farming was so unproductive that the USSR had to import grain from the West.
  • Political Repression: While Khrushchev sought "peaceful coexistence," Brezhnev reinstated terror and jailed dissidents.

3.7 Regional Conflicts: Afghanistan & Cambodia

In late 1979, the Soviets faced their own "Vietnam" in Afghanistan, while Cambodia spiraled into genocide.

  • Afghanistan (1979–1989): The USSR invaded to support a communist government, facing a 10-year battle against US-backed insurgents.
  • The Khmer Rouge: Led by Pol Pot, they unleashed a reign of terror in Cambodia until Vietnam invaded in 1979 to end the genocide.
  • Justice: International Tribunals were later established to try Khmer Rouge leaders for crimes against humanity.

3.8 Gorbachev’s Age of Reform (1985–1991)

Mikhail Gorbachev sought to reform—not collapse—the Soviet system through three revolutionary pillars.

Glasnost (Openness)

Allowed public criticism, local control, and the publication of new history books and intellectual works.

Perestroika (Restructuring)

Introduced private ownership, decentralization, and foreign investment to fix the stagnant economy.

Demokratizatsiya

Encouraged political participation through secret ballots, multiple candidates, and 10-year term limits.

Economic Reality

By 1988, the USSR had only 10,000 personal computers while the US had millions, highlighting the tech gap.

Despite these efforts, by 1991 the empire disintegrated, the Berlin Wall was torn down, and the Soviet Union dissolved into independent states.

Unit 4: Decolonization

4.1 Precolonial Ghana: Kingdoms and Trade

  • Home to several successful kingdoms like the Dogomba and the Ashanti (who settled near the forest line).
  • By the 15th century, substantial trade was conducted with sub-Saharan peoples to the north.

4.2 European Arrival & The Gold Coast

  • The Portuguese arrived in 1482 and built a fort at Elmina; the region became a major provider of gold for Europe.
  • By the 16th century, the slave trade had become a major interest; 20% of enslaved people taken from Africa were exported through this region.

4.3 Post-WWII Fervor & The Road to Sovereignty

  • Nationalist fervor grew after World War II, and local legislative council elections were held as early as 1925.
  • The UK eventually allowed more self-government in response to mounting internal pressure.

4.4 Kwame Nkrumah & Transition to Power

  • Nkrumah’s **Convention People's Party (CPP)** became the dominant political organization; he became Premier after a 1951 constitution change.
  • On March 6, 1957, the Gold Coast gained independence and was renamed **Ghana** in honor of the ancient empire.

4.5 The Shift to African Socialism

  • Nkrumah’s desire for a centralized "African Socialist" state met with dissension; he began treating critics harshly.
  • By 1960, Ghana became a republic with Nkrumah as President, but he began outlawing other political parties.

4.6 Economic Turmoil & Debt

  • Falling cacao prices and poorly financed development projects caused the economy to suffer.
  • By 1966, Ghana was deep in debt, and Nkrumah was eventually overthrown.

4.7 Independence Impacts on Africa

  • Ghana was the first African country to gain independence from Britain; its success and struggle served as a blueprint for the rest of the continent.

Unit 5: India & China

5.1 Trouble in the Chinese Republic (1911–1925)

  • Sun Yixian (father of modern China) struggled with warlords who seized provincial power; the cultural May Fourth Movement challenged traditions.
  • Sun accepted aid from the Soviet Union to build the **Guomindang (Nationalists)** into a modern force.

5.2 Mao Zedong's Communist Theory

  • Mao was a young revolutionary from peasant origins; he believed communism should seek support from the peasant masses rather than the small working class.

5.3 The Long March (1934–1935)

  • Nationalist leader Jiang Jieshi led "extermination campaigns" against the communists (the "Red bandits").
  • Mao and 100,000 followers led a year-long retreat known as the **Long March**; only 20,000 survived through guerrilla tactics.

5.4 Japanese Invasions & The Rape of Nanjing

  • Japan invaded Manchuria in 1931; in 1937, they struck China's cultural center in the **"Rape of Nanjing,"** killing hundreds of thousands of civilians.
  • The invasion forced a temporary alliance between the Guomindang and the Communists to fight the invaders.

5.5 Indian National Congress & Nationalism (1885)

  • Formed in 1885 by middle-class Indians to seek self-rule; the **Muslim League (1906)** was created later to protect Muslim minority rights.
  • The 1919 **Amritsar Massacre** (400 killed) turned the movement toward full independence.

5.6 Mahatma Gandhi & Satyagraha

  • Gandhi promoted **Ahimsa** (nonviolence) and **Satyagraha** (soul force); he encouraged civil disobedience inspired by Thoreau.

5.7 The Salt March (1930)

  • In 1930, Gandhi led a 240-mile march to the sea to protest the British monopoly on salt; 60,000 marchers were arrested.
  • He promoted the **spinning wheel** and the dhoti to reject British textiles and restore national pride.

5.8 Modern Indian Economy & Growth

  • India establishment democratic governance after 1947; the **Green Revolution** solved food security issues via technology.
  • Today, India is a global tech hub with a highly educated IT sector, though it still faces poverty across a high rural population.

Unit 6: Middle East

6.1 Ottoman Challenges & Transitions

  • The once-powerful Ottoman Empire faced decline due to internal ethnic diversity and external European pressure.
  • Post-war regional changes led to the formation of new national identities and modern state borders.

Unit 7: Latin America

7.1 Cuban Revolution & Fidel Castro

  • Fidel Castro & Che Guevara: Overthrew the Batista dictatorship (1959); Castro nationalized US assets, causing an immediate embargo.
  • Bay of Pigs (1961): A failed CIA-backed invasion by exiles that pushed Cuba closer to the Soviet Union.

7.2 Missile Crisis Impact

  • The threat of Soviet nuclear bases on the island led to a US naval blockade by Kennedy; it was the closest the world came to nuclear war.
